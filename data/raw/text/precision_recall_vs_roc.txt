PRECISION-RECALL CURVES VERSUS ROC CURVES

Precision-recall curves and ROC curves are both standard tools for evaluating binary
classifiers, but they serve different analytical purposes and excel under different
conditions. A thorough understanding of both, including when to use each and how to
interpret the differences, is essential for rigorous model selection and deployment
decision-making in machine learning pipelines.


PRECISION AND RECALL: DEFINITIONS

Precision measures what fraction of all positive predictions made by the classifier are
actually positive. It is computed as the number of true positives divided by the total
number of predicted positives. High precision means that when the model says positive, it
is usually right. Low precision means the model produces many false alarms.

Recall, also called sensitivity or the true positive rate, measures what fraction of all
actual positive instances are retrieved by the classifier. It is the number of true
positives divided by the total number of actual positives. High recall means the model
finds most of the positive cases. Low recall means many positives are missed.

The precision-recall trade-off is fundamental: raising the classification threshold
increases precision but reduces recall, because fewer predictions are made positive but
those that are tend to be more reliable. Lowering the threshold increases recall by
capturing more true positives at the cost of also accepting more false positives, which
reduces precision.


THE PRECISION-RECALL CURVE

Like the ROC curve, the precision-recall curve is constructed by sweeping the
classification threshold from high to low and recording the precision and recall at each
threshold. The result is a curve in the precision-recall plane. A high-quality classifier
maintains both high precision and high recall across a broad range of thresholds, producing
a curve that bows toward the upper-right corner of the plot.

A perfect classifier achieves precision of 1.0 and recall of 1.0 simultaneously for some
threshold, placing the curve at the upper-right corner. A random classifier produces a
curve that lies near the horizontal line representing the base rate, the fraction of
positive instances in the dataset.


AREA UNDER THE PRECISION-RECALL CURVE

The Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve, often abbreviated AP for Average Precision, is
computed as a numerical summary of the curve. It is commonly estimated as the weighted
mean of precisions at each threshold, with the weight given by the increase in recall from
the previous threshold. For perfectly ranked outputs, AP equals 1.0. For random outputs,
AP equals the base rate, the fraction of positive samples in the dataset.

A key property of AP is that its baseline value depends on class prevalence. With 10
percent positive samples, a random classifier achieves AP of approximately 0.10 regardless
of dataset size. This sensitivity to class balance is both a strength and a weakness: it
makes AP a demanding and informative metric for rare-event problems, but it also makes AP
scores incomparable across datasets with different prevalence rates.


WHEN ROC IS MORE APPROPRIATE

ROC curves and AUC are well suited for classification problems where both classes have
roughly equal importance and the dataset is reasonably balanced. Because false positive
rate is computed over the actual negative population, it remains stable even as the
evaluation set grows, making AUC useful for comparing classifier ranking quality across
experiments with consistent score distributions.

ROC AUC is also appropriate when the downstream task involves ranking or prioritization
rather than binary decision-making. In information retrieval, recommender systems, and
risk scoring, the relative ordering of predictions matters more than the absolute
calibration of probability values at any particular threshold. AUC directly measures this
ranking quality.


WHEN PRECISION-RECALL IS MORE APPROPRIATE

Precision-recall analysis is more informative than ROC analysis when the positive class
is rare relative to the negative class, a situation common in medical diagnosis, fraud
detection, anomaly identification, and document retrieval. In highly imbalanced settings,
AUC can be misleadingly optimistic.

The reason is that false positive rate is computed over the negative population. When
negatives are overwhelmingly common, even a large absolute number of false positives
translates to a small false positive rate. The ROC curve therefore looks favorable even
when the classifier produces far more false positives than true positives in absolute
terms. Precision, which is computed over all predicted positives, directly reflects
this absolute cost by dropping when false positives are numerous.

As a concrete illustration, consider a dataset with 99 negatives and 1 positive. A
classifier that flags 10 instances as positive, including the 1 true positive, achieves
recall of 1.0 and a false positive rate of only 9/99, approximately 0.09. The ROC curve
point looks excellent. However, precision is only 1/10, meaning 90 percent of its
positive predictions are wrong, which precision-recall analysis exposes immediately.


F1 SCORE AND THRESHOLD SELECTION

The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall and provides a single-number
summary of the precision-recall trade-off at a specific threshold. It equals zero when
either precision or recall is zero and achieves its maximum of 1.0 when both are perfect.

The harmonic mean penalizes extreme imbalance between precision and recall more severely
than the arithmetic mean would. A classifier with recall of 0.99 and precision of 0.01
achieves an arithmetic mean of 0.5 but an F1 score of approximately 0.02, correctly
reflecting its very poor precision.

Plotting F1 as a function of threshold allows selection of the operating point that
maximizes the harmonic balance between precision and recall. This threshold selection
procedure is equivalent to finding the point on the precision-recall curve with the
highest F1 value.


F-BETA SCORE FOR ASYMMETRIC COSTS

When precision and recall should not be weighted equally, the F-beta score generalizes
F1 by introducing a parameter beta that controls the relative importance of recall versus
precision. With beta greater than 1, recall is weighted more heavily. With beta less than
1, precision is favored. F2 and F0.5 are common choices in retrieval and filtering
applications where one error type is notably costlier than the other.


PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATION

For balanced classification problems, ROC AUC is a good default metric. For imbalanced
problems, especially those involving rare positive events, precision-recall AP provides a
more honest and practically meaningful evaluation. Using both metrics in parallel, plotting
both curves for candidate models, gives the most complete picture of classifier behavior
and supports more confident deployment decisions.
