KMEANS CLUSTERING: OVERVIEW AND MECHANICS

KMeans is one of the most widely used clustering algorithms in machine learning, valued for
its simplicity, computational efficiency, and ease of interpretation. It partitions a
dataset into a fixed number of clusters by iteratively assigning points to the nearest
centroid and updating centroid positions until convergence. Despite its age and simplicity,
KMeans remains a strong baseline in exploratory data analysis, document clustering, vector
quantization, and preprocessing for downstream supervised models.


CORE OBJECTIVE

The goal of KMeans is to partition n data points into k clusters such that the total
within-cluster variance is minimized. Formally, the algorithm minimizes the sum of squared
distances between each point and its assigned cluster centroid. This objective is often
called inertia or the within-cluster sum of squares. A lower inertia indicates tighter,
more compact clusters, though it must be interpreted carefully since inertia always
decreases as k increases, even when adding clusters provides no real structure.


ALGORITHMIC STEPS

KMeans begins by selecting k initial centroid positions. In the standard formulation, these
are chosen randomly from among the data points, though more sophisticated initialization
strategies exist. The algorithm then alternates between two steps until the assignments
stabilize or a maximum iteration count is reached.

In the assignment step, every data point is assigned to the cluster whose centroid is
closest in Euclidean distance. This creates k groups, each consisting of all points closer
to that group's centroid than to any other.

In the update step, each centroid is repositioned to the arithmetic mean of all points
currently assigned to it. If no points are assigned to a centroid, various strategies
apply, ranging from reinitializing that centroid randomly to removing the empty cluster
entirely.

The algorithm converges when no point changes its cluster assignment between iterations,
meaning all centroids are stable. In practice, convergence is fast, often within 10 to 50
iterations for well-structured data.


SENSITIVITY TO INITIALIZATION

A well-known limitation of KMeans is its sensitivity to the initial centroid placement.
Because the algorithm performs gradient descent in a non-convex loss landscape, it can
converge to local minima that do not represent the globally optimal partition. Poor
initialization can result in clusters that are clearly wrong, such as a centroid being
placed in the gap between two dense groups rather than inside one.

Running the algorithm multiple times with different random initializations and selecting the
result with the lowest inertia is the standard mitigation strategy. Most implementations
default to running KMeans 10 times and returning the best result. The number of such runs
is often exposed as a tunable parameter.

KMeans++ initialization, which places initial centroids with probability proportional to
their squared distance from already-chosen centroids, produces significantly more reliable
and consistent results than pure random initialization. It tends to select centroids that
are spread across the data, reducing the chance of two centroids starting near the same
dense region.


INERTIA AND THE ELBOW METHOD

Inertia is the primary diagnostic metric for KMeans. It measures how tightly packed the
clusters are by summing the squared distance from each point to its assigned centroid.
Inertia is always non-negative and decreases monotonically as k increases. When k equals
the number of data points, inertia reaches zero because every point is its own cluster.

The elbow method exploits this behavior by plotting inertia as a function of k and looking
for a point where the rate of decrease changes sharply. This kink in the curve, resembling
the bend of an elbow, suggests that adding more clusters beyond this point provides
diminishing returns in terms of compactness. The value of k at the elbow is a reasonable
estimate of the true number of clusters in the data.

In practice, the elbow is not always sharp or unambiguous. When data lacks clear cluster
structure or when clusters have very different sizes, the inertia curve may decline smoothly
with no obvious knee. In such cases, supplementary metrics and domain knowledge are
necessary.


SILHOUETTE SCORE AS A COMPLEMENT

The silhouette score measures how similar each point is to its own cluster compared to the
nearest neighboring cluster. Values range from -1 to 1, where values close to 1 indicate
well-separated, compact assignments, values near 0 indicate boundary cases, and negative
values indicate likely misassignment.

Plotting silhouette scores for a range of k values alongside inertia provides a more
complete picture of cluster quality. The optimal k often coincides with both a silhouette
peak and an inertia elbow, giving greater confidence in the selection.


GEOMETRIC ASSUMPTIONS AND LIMITATIONS

KMeans implicitly assumes that clusters are convex, roughly spherical, and of similar size.
These assumptions arise from its use of Euclidean distance and centroid averaging. When
clusters are elongated, non-convex, or vastly different in density, KMeans fails to capture
their structure.

A classic demonstration involves two concentric circles or two crescent shapes. KMeans will
always split the data with a linear boundary, assigning half of each ring to each centroid,
while DBSCAN cleanly separates the rings by density. This limitation is visible
immediately in scatter plot comparisons: KMeans centroids often sit in locations that do
not represent any actual population center when data geometry is non-trivial.

Another limitation is that KMeans requires all features to be numeric and on comparable
scales. Including a feature measured in thousands alongside one measured in fractions gives
disproportionate influence to the high-scale feature in distance calculations. Feature
standardization before clustering is therefore essential.


APPLICATIONS IN ML PIPELINES

KMeans is frequently used as a preprocessing step in larger pipelines. In image
compression, it reduces color palettes by clustering pixel values. In natural language
processing, it groups document embeddings into topic clusters. In retrieval systems, it
creates a coarse inverted index over vector space, enabling fast approximate nearest
neighbor search through cluster-level filtering before exhaustive search within a candidate
cluster.

In a multimodal RAG system, KMeans over embedding vectors can provide a structured
partitioning of the knowledge base, allowing the retrieval component to first identify
which cluster is most relevant to a query and then search within that cluster, significantly
reducing retrieval latency at scale.
